Rasnik patterns

The ATLAS muon spectrometer determines the momentum of muons that are created in collisions by running the muons through a magnetic field and measuring their path. Its alignment is necessary for accurate measurements; since high-energy muons only have a slight change in trajectory in the magnetic field, the spectrometer must be very precisely aligned. As this level of precision cannot be achieved through human means, rasnik masks are used to precisely align the small wheels of the ATLAS muon spectrometer.


A rasnik pattern (top left) is painted onto a piece of glass or copper, called the rasnik mask. It consists of a black and white checkerboard pattern with some squares inverted. The inverted sections of the image tell us which part of the mask the image is focused on - the rasnik position. The mask is placed on the lens with a source of light behind it. In this case, the lens is a fiber optic plate and the light source is a piece of glow tape. A picture is taken with the CCD camera and processed using the LWDAQ software. Using the rasnik position, we are able to find the coordinates and can determine the change in position to a fraction of a micron.
